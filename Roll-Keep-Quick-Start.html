Roll&Keep Quick Start

Get Started!

Remember those books where the teenage wizards struggle against the Dark Lord of Evil? That movie where the dwarves fight to recapture their mountain home from a dragon? That animated TV show about mystical knights and their army of clones righting wrongs across the galaxy?

Aren’t those awesome? Here’s your chance to put yourself in the heroes’ shoes in stories like those.

Roll&Keep is a tabletop roleplaying game, where you and your friends gather around and tell stories full of danger, excitement, and adventure. You might have played games similar to this before—Dungeons & Dragons is a very popular one—but don’t worry if you haven’t; this quick start will guide you through the basics.

Here’s what you’ll need to play:

  • A few friends. One of you will be the gamemaster, the others players.
  • Polyhedral dice with different numbers of sides, a few of each. You can buy these dice at a gaming store or online.
  • Paper to use for characters sheets and notes, a few sheets for each player.
  • Index cards or sticky notes or similar slips of paper.
  • Tokens for plot points. These can be poker chips, beads, pennies, or anything similar.

Create Your Character

  • Name and appearance. Describe your character’s appearance and give them a name.
  • Distinction. First, decide on your character’s distinction and assign it . This is a single phrase or sentence that neatly sums up your character, saying who you are, what you do, what your “deal” is. When you think about your distinction, try to think of two things: how this trait could help you, and how it might make things harder for you. Good distinction traits do both. Distinctions are the universal trait set and regular Roll&Keep games have three distinctions.
  • Trait Sets. This quick start has one trait set, Attributes: assign between your Physical, Mental, and Social attributes. Regular Roll&Keep games have at least two prime trait sets, of varying types (e.g. attributes, skills, affiliations, relationships, powers, roles, values), and several temporary trait sets (e.g. assets, complications).
  • Plot Points. You start the game with 1 PP.

Taking Action, Rolling the Dice

In a game of Roll&Keep, you will control the actions of the character you created, contributing to the story you are all telling together. In general, the GM will narrate the world and the actions of non-player characters (NPCs), and the other players will narrate their individual PCs’ actions.

To act, follow the principle of fiction first: say what your character is trying to do, then figure out how you’ll do that in the system. Your character’s Distination and Attributes inform what they can attempt and help set the context for interpreting the results. Most people couldn’t even try to perform emergency surgery on a disemboweled ally, but with a Distinction establishing a medical background, you can try. Without that Distinction you might at best buy a few moments for some last words. When in doubt, check with your GM and the table.

How do you know if you’re successful? Often, your character will simply succeed, because the action isn’t hard and nobody’s trying to stop you. But in difficult or unpredictable situations, you’ll break out the dice to find out what happens.

When a character wants to take an action, the group should think about these questions:

  • What’s stopping this from happening?
  • What could go wrong?
  • How is it interesting when it does go wrong?

If no one has good answers to all of these questions, it simply happens. Driving to the airport doesn’t require a roll of the dice. Racing down the highway to a waiting plane while being pursued by cybernetically enhanced beasts from another world, on the other hand, is a perfect time to roll the dice.

Whenever you take action, follow these steps:

  1. The gamemaster (GM) decides the difficulty of the task based on the situation, rolls, and totals the two dice.

  2. The player choose the attribute that fits the task and add two dice of that type to the pool. Regular Roll&Keep games have at least two prime trait sets, but this quick start only has one, so add it to the dice pool twice.
  3. If your distinction is relevant, add to the dice pool.
  4. If you want, spend a PP to add a asset to the pool.
  5. Roll your dice pool, and add together the two highest dice.
  6. If you exceed the GMs total describe how you succeed; otherwise the GM describes what happens instead.
  7. If you roll any ones, then these are called hitches and don't count in your total, but instead earn a PP.
